How We Built Pokemine

A kids' guide to building things with AI - featuring Gyatt, Tacoloak, and Socko

Pokemine is the app where you type a silly idea and get a real Pokemon card. Dad and an AI built it together in one day. Here is how - and the three big skills that made it work.

Skill 1: Architecture (plan the LEGO build before you dump out the bricks)

Before writing ANY code, we spent a long time just deciding what to build. That's called architecture - it's the blueprint.

  • We asked questions first: Who's using it? (You, on your Chromebook.) What must it do? (Draw creatures, evolve them, print cards.)
  • We drew the plan as small pieces that each do ONE job: one piece saves Pokemon to folders, one piece talks to the art AI, one piece draws the card on screen. Small pieces are easy to fix and easy to swap.
  • Coolest trick: we made the "artist" a plug. The app doesn't care WHICH AI draws the picture - there's a paid super-artist (fast, fancy), a free one that works through Dad's browser, and even a practice one that draws nothing (for testing without spending money). Same plug, different artists.
Big idea: decide the shape of the thing before building it, and make the parts swappable - like LEGO bricks, not glue.

Skill 2: Context Engineering (the AI only knows what you tell it)

An AI is like a super-fast new kid at school: really capable, but it doesn't know YOUR rules until you explain them. Feeding it the right information is called context engineering.

  • We taught it the style: "cel-shaded, like Ken Sugimori's watercolor art, 1-2 main colors, white background". Before that, art came out random. After, everything matched.
  • We taught it Pokemon naming: real names are two words squished together (Odd + radish = Oddish, Macho + champ = Machamp). Then it invented Tacoloak (taco + cloak) that evolves into Tacolossus (taco + colossus). See how the "Taco" stays and the ending levels up?
  • We built a "canon pack" - a cheat sheet of Pokemon universe facts (the Arceus creation myth, how Pokedex entries sound) that gets slipped into EVERY request. That's why Socko's backstory mentions the Johto region like a real legend.
Big idea: when the AI's answer is bad, the fix is usually better instructions, not a better AI.

Skill 3: Building with AI (helpers, checkers, and trying again)

One AI didn't build this alone. It worked like a construction crew:

  • The architect AI made plans and checked everyone's work (that was the boss AI Dad talked to).
  • Builder AIs got very exact instructions - down to the actual code - and built one piece at a time.
  • Checkers ran constantly. We wrote little tests - robot referees - that yell if anything breaks. Every change had to keep all referees happy (we ended with 16 of them).
  • Nobody was trusted on their word. When a builder said "done!", the boss re-ran the tests itself and looked at real screenshots. Trust, but verify. Mostly verify.

Best story of the day: to make the free artist work, we needed to sneak a picture out of a web page and into the app. Plan 1 failed (the computer blocked the Downloads folder). Plan 2 failed (the image link needed a password). Plan 3 failed (the browser blocked the shortcut). Plan 4 worked: the "copy image" button plus the clipboard - the same copy-paste you use every day. That's real engineering: three fails, zero quitting, one win.

Big idea: professionals don't avoid failing - they fail fast, learn WHY, and try a different door.

Skill 4: Watching real players (that's you!)

The app passed every robot-referee test... and then real kids used it and found problems no referee caught.

  • Someone typed a new name into the Redraw box - and the AI painted the name INTO the picture instead of renaming the card. Oops. Fix: we now tell the artist "NO text in the image, ever", and renaming is tap-the-name-on-the-card.
  • The loading messages ("Bribing a Snorlax off the road...") turned out to be a favorite thing. So we wrote 160 of them, packed with real Pokemon lore - and put them in their own file so ANYONE can add more without touching the app's code.
  • Errors and costs now get written into a logbook file, because "I saw an error yesterday" is only useful if yesterday got written down.
Big idea: tests catch the mistakes you imagined. Players find the ones you didn't. Watching people use your thing IS part of building it.

The receipts

  • Gyatt, The Bouncy Bottom Pokemon - first ever created, evolved into royal Gyattress (the crown survived evolution because we showed the AI its old picture).
  • Tacolossus - proof the naming lessons worked. Signature move: Guac Slam.
  • Socko, The stinky sock - made for FREE through the browser bridge, with a folk-legend backstory from the canon pack.
  • The whole thing cost about one dollar of AI art, and every Pokemon you make is saved forever in Google Drive.

Skill 5: Checking someone else's plan (two AIs grade each other's homework)

For the big evolutions update, one AI wrote the plan and a DIFFERENT AI checked it before we built anything. Like trading papers in class - you catch way more mistakes in someone else's work.

  • The checker didn't just read the plan and nod. It did the math itself - and found the plan's fix for a wonky card layout was wrong. The plan said "nudge it a little more"; the math said "that nudge lands in the exact same broken spot."
  • Then the FIRST AI checked the checker back... and found a small mistake in the checker's homework too! Nobody's perfect. Checking each other is how the answer got right.
  • The bug itself was sneaky: in CSS, "3em" means "3 times as big as the text next to it." The tiny label had tiny text, so its "3em" was a SHORT hop. The big name had big text, so its "3em" was a LONG hop. Same instruction, different jumps - that's why they didn't line up! Fix: put them both in one box and move the box.
Big idea: don't just ask "does this sound right?" - grab a pencil and check the numbers. Sounding right and BEING right are different things.

The new evolution rules (and the math inside them)

Every stage is now its own real card: its own 4-digit number, its own backstory that continues the last one, and its own "Born from" words.

Fences made of arrays. Each stage has a power fence written as a pair of numbers, [lowest, highest]:

  • Stage 1 HP: [30, 120]   Stage 2: [60, 160]   Stage 3: [90, 200]
  • The fences climb! Evolving always means stronger - and the app FORCES it: if the AI rolls a weak number, the app pushes it back inside the fence and makes sure it beats the old card by at least 10.

The 5% jackpot. When a Pokemon evolves for the last time, the app picks a secret random number between 0 and 1 - like throwing a dart at a number line.

  • Lands below 0.030 → VMAX (3 out of every 100 tries - silver!)
  • Lands between 0.030 and 0.045 → EX (1.5 out of 100 - rarer, gold!)
  • Lands between 0.045 and 0.050 → MEGA (only 1 in 200 - the super jackpot!)
  • Add them up: 3% + 1.5% + 0.5% = 5%. That's 1 in 20 final evolutions. Rare enough to scream about.

Jackpot cards get their own bigger fences too - a MEGA is [260, 340], so even the weakest MEGA beats the strongest normal card (200). Plus a glowing rainbow frame, a MEGA badge, and extra-epic art.

Big idea: random doesn't mean unfair. We chose the exact odds with math, so rare things feel AMAZING but still happen. (No re-rolling! The dart throws once.)

Skill 6: Making it faster (and never making you wait for nothing)

For a while, the app had a mean habit. If the artist was slow, you'd stare at a blank screen for up to two whole minutes... and then it would give up and show you nothing. Two minutes of waiting for zero Pokemon. That's the worst deal ever. So we fixed it.

  • Show progress as Poke Balls. Instead of one boring "loading...", the screen now swaps the ball while it works. A regular Poke Ball while the AI thinks up your creature ("Sending your idea to the Professor..."), then an Ultra Ball while the artist draws ("Drawing your Pokemon..."), then a flashy Master Ball when it's done. It's like a progress bar, except the bar IS a Pokemon.
  • Never come back empty-handed. If the PICTURE fails for some reason, you still get the CARD, with a blank picture spot, plus a friendly note: "Caught it... almost! The picture got away." Then you just tap Redraw to retry the picture, not the whole thing. Before, one bad picture threw away your whole creature.
  • The helper asks how long to wait. The free browser-artist used to give up after exactly two minutes every time, even if the drawing was almost finished. Now the server writes a little note on the job saying "you have this long", and the helper reads it. No more quitting too early on a slow-but-fine drawing.
  • The errors got friendly. Instead of scary "Error 503", it says things like "Professor Oak's computers are swamped, wait a sec and try again." Same problem, way less panic.

One more reuse of an old trick: the words AI is now a plug too, just like the artist plug from Skill 1. So if one word-AI ever stops working, we point the plug at a backup and the app keeps going. The lesson again: make the parts swappable like LEGO, not glued.

Big idea: a loading screen that shows you WHERE it is feels way faster than one that doesn't, even if it takes the same time. And always giving the player SOMETHING (even a card missing its picture) beats giving them nothing.
